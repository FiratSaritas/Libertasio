Vera C. Rubin Observatory Prepares for First Light

Published on July 16, 2025

CERRO PACHÓN, CHILE — The NSF–DOE Vera C. Rubin Observatory, a groundbreaking astronomical facility formerly known as the Large Synoptic Survey Telescope (LSST), is entering its final phase of construction atop Cerro Pachón in northern Chile.

Featuring the 8.4 m Simonyi Survey Telescope paired with the world’s largest digital camera — a 3.2 gigapixel behemoth — the observatory will scan the southern sky every few nights and conduct the ambitious Legacy Survey of Space and Time (LSST), a ten-year mission to capture a celestial "time‑lapse movie" of the universe.

The telescope’s innovative three‑mirror design delivers razor-sharp images over a 3.5° field of view, enabling it to cover enormous swaths of sky quickly. With exposures repeated every few nights, each patch of sky will be imaged roughly 800 times over the survey’s lifespan.

The data troves — expected to total hundreds of petabytes — will advance key science goals: mapping dark matter and energy, cataloging Solar System bodies, tracking transient events like supernovae, and unveiling the structure of the Milky Way.

The observatory recorded its first on-sky images with an engineering camera in October 2024, and system "first light" images were released on June 23, 2025. Full survey operations are expected to begin in late 2025 after final testing.

Named in honor of Vera C. Rubin — a pioneer whose work provided key evidence for dark matter — the observatory also stands as the first major U.S. astronomical facility named after a woman. It is operated under NSF NOIRLab in cooperation with SLAC and the Department of Energy.

As the final construction wraps and the world awaits the first data releases, astronomers anticipate a transformative leap forward — one that could reshape our understanding of the cosmos.
